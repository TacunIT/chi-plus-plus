Being obsessive-compulsive with a slight tendency toward paranoia, if you make a living as a security expert, is a good thing; the same personality traits, on the other hand, are decidedly a bad thing when, at 9:55 PM, your housekeeper texts you: "I took a rapid test and it came back positive".
You won't be able to get a swab test done before 8:00 AM the next day, so you know at least ten hours of controlled panic await you; a few less, if you manage to fall asleep.
You try to distract yourself by watching TV, but yet another Jason Statham thriller, interspersed with ads for anti-anxiety medication (a conflict of interest you promise yourself to look into more closely, if you survive), only adds to your agitation; so, you turn off the TV, make yourself a calming tea, read a bit, and then try to sleep.
As soon as you close your eyes, though, your brain starts doing what it does best, that is, cyclically running through every possible consequence of a given event; except this time, what might be infected isn't a computer, it's you.
Good news: you have no symptoms. Bad news: you only saw your housekeeper four days ago, so the game is still open.
