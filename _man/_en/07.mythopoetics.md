There are two theories about me: some think I went mad trying to *debug* the Universe, others think I decided to *debug* the Universe because I'm mad.

Master Canaro too was accused of being mad when he said God had tasked him with *porting* the Universe from COBOL to C++, but he wasn't mad.<br />
I've kept these documents: they are the last page of his diary and the reports that were sent to the company he worked for while he was on assignment to the "Client".<br />
Put them at the beginning of your book, so that everything we say afterward will be nothing but a repetition.

Once the retraining course was over, Master Canaro was never the same again.<br />
He went back to his company, but stayed only long enough to hand in his resignation.<br />
He founded our Order and began preaching the mysticism of programming, immediately gaining a large following.<br />
The time was ripe for change: there had been the prodigious growth of the end of the millennium, and there had been the ruinous crash of March 2000. The old guard was tired of depending on the whims of the stock market and of marketing; the young wanted the Promised Land for which they had abandoned classical studies; users just wanted their email to work.<br />
Master Canaro's doctrine was simple: bring computing back into an ivory tower, accept its imponderable component, and raise it from a productive activity to a mystical one.<br />
Enough with improvisation, enough with *time to market*, enough with unreliable products: whoever wanted to write code had to enter a monastic Order and follow a long path of initiation before they could begin working.<br />
Many saw in Master Canaro "the wind that would sweep away the mud": the old guard was delighted to see its status as a chosen caste recognized once again; the young, who were looking for shelter, found it within the monasteries; users learned that their email would work, and were happy.<br />
There was also a political component that favored the success of the doctrine preached by Master Canaro. Society had by then become entirely dependent on computers, and computers depended on computer scientists.<br />
If they had ever decided to band together and go on strike, they could have brought the country, if not the whole world, to its knees.<br />
The psychopathologies typical of computer scientists, their inability to manage — or even to *conceive of* — a social life, made this hypothesis quite unlikely, but it was still too great a risk to ignore.<br />
On the contrary, the indoctrination of computer scientists, their segregation into a caste with strong mystical-religious overtones, would keep them away from the temptations of trade unions and would make the prospect of a strike as improbable as a strike of priests.

Master Canaro's teachings never strayed beyond the realm of software.<br />
He never once mentioned *hardware* in any of his writings; he used to say that if a man configures a *firewall* he is a computer scientist, if he connects two cables he is an electrician.<br />
Once I asked Master Canaro why he had never given any guidance on the physical reliability of servers.<br />
"If it comes to that," he answered, "I never produced any tables for calculating reinforced concrete either." Only years later did I understand what he meant.<br />
Despite his lack of interest in what he called "real things", the cultural revolution brought about by Master Canaro had consequences for computer manufacturers too.<br />
Most of them were slow to perceive the changes underway and paid dearly for their inattention; the others thrived.<br />
Even though Master Canaro never took a definite side in the dispute that pitted supporters of the doctrine of the *Great Vehicle* (the *mainframes*) against those who instead favored the *Small Vehicle* (home PCs), some high prelates declared themselves in favor of mainframe monotheism, and in the end the supporters of polytheism were defeated.<br />
In offices and homes, PCs were replaced by terminals, and every building, besides the garage and the boiler room, now had a server room.<br />
The territory was divided into dioceses, and each diocese was entrusted to a deacon whose task was to oversee the correct functioning of the various systems.<br />
Although those of us who had been close to him since the beginning of his preaching knew that C++ was his favorite programming language, Master Canaro let Java become the community's official language, quietly enduring the attacks of those who accused him of having sold out to Sun.<br />
The existing procedures were all rewritten; the monastic orders that taught C++ became fewer and fewer, until eventually there were none left.<br />
It was only then that Master Canaro spoke to me for the first time about
*C'hi++*.
