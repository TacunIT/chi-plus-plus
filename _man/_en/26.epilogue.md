It was one of my fellow brothers who came to tell me.
He opened the door of my cell without even knocking, a black silhouette against the light of the corridor behind him.
"The old man's taken ill, they've brought him to the infirmary," he said. "You'd better hurry if you want to say goodbye, I don't think he has much time left."
I should have been grateful to him, for thinking to warn me, but instead I hated him, both for the news and for his grammar mistake.
I got up and dressed in the dark, thinking about how clever the Benedictines are, sleeping in their habits, never having to pollute the drama of such moments with thoughts like "Where did I put my underwear?"
As soon as I had something on, I ran out and, barefoot, went down to the infirmary.
I hesitated before opening the door, just as I had hesitated in front of his cell door the very first time I'd gone looking for him.
When I finally worked up the courage and opened it, I saw him lying on one of the beds at the far end of the room.
He was barely a ripple on the surface of the blanket, but the blanket &mdash; I noticed, with some relief &mdash; was moving slightly, following the rhythm of his breath.
He noticed my presence (or perhaps he already knew I'd come) and, in a faint voice, said:
"Well, well! Look who's here: Nino the student.."
He was dying, this was probably the last time we'd see each other, and he was greeting me with a line from a Nino D'Angelo movie.
I hated him too, for a moment; just long enough to understand why he'd done it.
"Are you happy now?" the Master asked me.
"About what?" I asked, and added: "No, I'm not happy. Why would I be happy?"
The Master took two breaths before answering.
"You've got your book, haven't you? I've taught you everything I know; I'm of no more use."
I didn't understand: did he mean he was dying because of me?
"I don't understand," I said. "Do you mean you're dying because of me?"
(I don't know if you've noticed, but when someone we love is dying, our thoughts and sentences tend to become less literary and more basic.)
"Everything I've taught you, did you think I was joking? We're here for a purpose; when we're no longer useful, we can be removed." He coughed, then went on: "The `delete` operator, remember? You have to free the memory, otherwise the program will have no room left to run."
Had I killed him? Really? I felt my heart pounding in my ears, and I eyed, with sudden greed, a sphygmomanometer sitting on one of the infirmary's tables; who knows what my blood pressure was.
"Heaven plays no favorites: to it, we are all precious and useless at the same time, like the Queen in a game of Chess. She's the most important piece, after the King, but a good player has no trouble sacrificing her, if it means winning the game."
He motioned for me to give him a little water; he drank, then started speaking again.
"Or maybe not. Has it ever crossed your mind that this whole theory could be wrong? That I might really be crazy, like your fellow brothers say?
Wittgenstein, at thirty, laid out a philosophy that he himself renounced ten years later, in favor of a new creed. If he'd lived another ten years, would he have changed his mind again?
The history of Humanity is dotted with bizarre ideas, each with its own fine following of believers; what makes you think C'hi++ is any different?
The fact that it can offer a rational and coherent answer to certain phenomena that would otherwise go unexplained doesn't necessarily mean it corresponds to the truth.
Yes, of course: its axioms seem to be confirmed by reality, but what does that even count for?
It could be us who fail to notice the errors.
We believed for hundreds of years that the Sun revolved around the Earth, and that too was a hypothesis backed by the facts, at least on the surface."
He smiled at my puzzled look, then went on speaking:
"Don't worry: I'm not crazy, but don't you go thinking that what I've taught you is science.
C'hi++ is neither science nor religion, even though it sometimes pretends to be one or the other.
It seeks the Truth, like science, and tries to give meaning to our existence, like religion, but it can't prove what it claims, like science, and it doesn't want you to accept it on faith, like religion.
The purpose of C'hi++ is to help us live better, so, what you should ask yourself isn't whether the theses of Spazionism are correct, but whether what I've taught you makes your life better or not."
He took another sip of water, then, with his eyes closed, said:
"Your master is dying and you're visibly grieving, so this is an excellent moment to test it. Go outside, look up, and ask yourself whether what I've taught you makes this moment any easier to face."
I objected that it didn't seem like the right time: it was raining, and I was barefoot, but that was only part of the problem: in truth, I didn't want to leave him, because it seemed to me his condition was rapidly getting worse.
"It's the last thing I'll ask of you. Are you really going to refuse me?"
I gave in: I went outside, into the rain, and looked up, as I'd been ordered to do.
The moon and the stars were hidden behind the clouds, and in any case the rain was hitting my eyes, keeping me from seeing clearly.
Even so, or perhaps precisely for that reason, I didn't lower my gaze, and let the raindrops mix with my tears.
That's how I understood.
Those drops of water that were now rain had, before, been water vapor, and sea, and river, and before that &mdash; for all I could know &mdash; blood, sweat, piss, ice, wine, muscle, plants, saliva, mud, lime, or concrete.
Whatever they had been bound to, in their previous existence, they were now water again, and ready for another cycle of life.
The heat of the sun had carried them up, now gravity was bringing them back down, toward the sea.
I went back to the Master's cell to tell him I understood, but the blanket wasn't moving anymore.
"Never mind," I thought. "I'll tell him next time."

This is the Master's funeral poem.
A "ready-made" funeral poem, as he called it, because the text is that of the funeral poem of the Zen monk Gesshu Soko.
The Master translated it into German when he discovered that the verb *zu treffen* can mean both *to meet* and *to hit the mark*.

<blockquote class="spalla-sx">
Einatmen, Ausatmen,<br />
Vorwärts gehen, Rückwärts gehen,<br />
Leben, sterben, kommen, gehen.<br />
Wie zwei Pfeile, die sich im Flug treffen.<br />
Mitten im Nichts,<br />
Eine Straße, die direkt zu meinem wirklichen Zuhause führt.
</blockquote>
