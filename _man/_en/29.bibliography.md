From a literary standpoint, the gestation of *C'hi++* began in the mid-1980s, when I bought
*Vijñāna Bhairava* and
*Zen in the Art of Archery*.
I knew nothing about Zen, at the time; I bought them almost as a joke, because the titles had caught my curiosity.
Reading the introduction of the first one, I discovered that, in the 7th century, there had been a dispute between two delegations of Buddhist philosophers, meant to settle a controversy over the correct way to reach Enlightenment, and that, at the end of the gathering, the losing side committed ritual suicide:

> some cutting themselves to pieces, some severing their own genitals, some setting themselves on fire

The text, though, turned out to be more complicated than I expected, so I set it aside.
Herrigel's book, by contrast, I read from cover to cover, and I liked it so much that I took up archery myself.

At the start of the 1990s, my friend Wayne Martini gave me a copy of
*Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance*, saying: "Read it. It changed my life."
I thought: "These Americans, always so dramatic!", but, as often happens, I was wrong: Pirsig's book &mdash; which I read right as my love affair with computers was beginning &mdash; changed my life too, and pushed me to dig deeper into Zen Buddhism.

In *Zen Flesh, Zen Bones*, I discovered that the origin of the fable of the *Runaway Horse*, which Calvino included in his *Italian Folktales*, was anything but Italian, while in *The Gateless Gate* I was struck by the Koan *Nansen Cuts the Cat in Two*, because I couldn't understand by what right the follower of a religion preaching loving-kindness would cut a cat in half, for purely didactic purposes.
It would take me a little over ten years to understand it.

In the meantime, thanks to archery, I had become friends with Claudio Munisso, with whom, in 1995, I wrote a programming manual covering C and C++.
He wrote the part on the C language, I wrote the one on C++.
The structure of the manual's chapters was borrowed from that of
*Gödel, Escher, Bach: An Eternal Golden Braid*, by Douglas Hofstadter, with a narrative preamble introducing the technical text.

> One day the Professor walked into the café and I, as always, went over to his table to take his order, but before I could even speak, he told me he was waiting for two friends, Mr. C. and Mr. D., and that he'd wait for them to arrive before ordering. These friends of his, he then explained, were coming by car from the other side of the city. Both knew how to drive, and both had a road atlas, but C. had a small problem: he was unable to work out driving directions except in terms specific to driving a car.

At the start of each chapter there was a quote; I don't remember the ones from the chapters I wrote myself, but I remember very well two lines Claudio picked, because they pushed me to go read the two authors quoted.
One was from *Meister Eckhart*:

> Take away the mind, and the eye will remain open, purposeless

The other was from Ananda Coomaraswamy:

> If usefulness and value are not in fact synonymous, it is only because, while the former implies effectiveness, value can also be attributed to something useless

The first book of Coomaraswamy's I read was *Il Grande Brivido*, and, ever since, everything I've said or written about art, here or elsewhere, has been nothing but a rehash of his ideas.
I also owe him my discovery of Attar's *Mantiq al-Tayr*.

Two books without which *C'hi++* would never have existed, or would have been very different, are: Musashi's *The Book of Five Rings*, given to me by a friend in the late '90s, and Poe's *Eureka*, which I bought after finding it cited by Calvino in *Six Memos for the Next Millennium*, together with *A Short History of Infinity*.

A book without which *I myself* would have been different is *The Selfish Gene*.
Dawkins may well be a *godless* man, as the Master calls him in the book, but he is an extremely intelligent one, who changed how I see the World, or at least part of it.
What surprises me about him is how someone so intelligent can fail to see that it's a terrible error of generalization to assume that anyone who isn't an atheist must necessarily be either a fanatic or a bigot.

My hope is that *C'hi++* can help clear up this misunderstanding.
