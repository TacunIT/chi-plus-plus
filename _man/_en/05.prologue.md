I was born into a wealthy family, but too late.<br />
As the second son, I watched my father's thriving business pass &mdash; by birthright, but also by natural inclination &mdash; to my identical twin older brothers, and, as is customary for younger sons, I was sent into monastic life.
I entered the seminary at the age of nine and took my vows on the day of my eighteenth birthday.
I met the Master four years later.<br />
I was sent to him because an old program, after nearly a decade of uninterrupted operation, had developed problems, and there was no one in the monastery who knew how to fix it.
Although I had spent two-thirds of my life in that same building, I had never met the Master before then.
I knew little about him &mdash; mainly that it was better not to know much &mdash; and I had never seen him, not in the refectory, nor at any celebration or emergency.
To all of us younger monks, he was a kind of mythological figure, malevolent or benevolent depending on which stories were told about her.<br />
I knocked on his door cautiously, half-expecting terrible reprisals for that unwarranted intrusion into his life, and just as cautiously accepted his invitation to enter.
The furnishings of our own rooms were simple and essential, but compared to the Master's cell, they seemed lavish.
There were no windows, and the only two sources of light were a small lamp and the screen of what looked like an old computer from the late twentieth century.
In one corner stood a curious sofa-bed, like a park bench, which must have served as his nightly resting place; on the opposite side of the room, a kind of bookshelf made from bricks and rough wooden planks held his few personal belongings.
Nothing covered the stone floor.

«My program is throwing errors.»<br />
I answered yes, even though it hadn't been a question.
The Master nodded.<br />
«It was trained to do that. We'll fix it now.»<br />
He began typing on his keyboard, and I moved closer, curious.
When I finally saw the image projected on the screen, I understood why no one in the monastery had been able to work on his program.<br />
«Yes, it's C++,» the Master said, sensing my curiosity. «It was once thought to be the language of the future. As is the case with every language, really.»<br />
I asked what he meant by saying the program had been trained to throw errors.<br />
«That's a foolish question. Then again, that you were telling the truth is obvious, since I didn't even ask what kind of flaw had been detected. Anyway, you may go tell your superior that the old madman has fixed the error.»<br />
I couldn't take my eyes off the screen; I was as if hypnotized by that strange code, which I could only partly understand.
In those sequences of instructions there was something I had never seen before: a rhythm, an indefinable beauty I had fallen in love with at first sight.<br />
I asked the Master to teach me C++.<br />
«Whatever for? There are far easier languages to learn.»<br />
I explained that my interest wasn't technological, but aesthetic.
He remained silent for a few seconds, considering what I had said, then asked:<br />
«What is the duty of a programmer?»<br />
I begged him to define his question more precisely.<br />
«Come back tomorrow. If you can tell me what the duty of a programmer is, I will teach you C++.»<br />

I spent the whole night meditating on that strange question, and the next morning I returned to the Master.
I said that the duty of a programmer was to write good code.
The Master did not even look away from the screen and said:<br />
«Come back tomorrow. If you can tell me what it means to write good code, I will teach you C++.»<br />

As one might easily guess, the next day, and for several days after, the Master found ways to keep postponing the start of my apprenticeship, asking ever more specific questions that struck at even the slightest gap in my answers.
I examined every possible aspect of software production, from system resource usage to the social implications of the employment growth that comes from software evolution &mdash; but it was no use: every time, the Master found a loophole to escape his promise.<br />
Finally, I couldn't take it anymore. Exasperated, I said I'd had enough of his stubborn quibbling: no matter how specific my answers, there would always be a margin of uncertainty.
If he wanted to teach me C++, he had to start that very day.<br />
The Master looked at me and, smiling, turned off the computer.<br />
«No, that's enough for today. Come back tomorrow for the second lesson.»

In recent months the Master's health has declined, and so I asked his permission to transcribe his teachings, so that I might pass them on in turn to other disciples.<br />
To my great surprise, he agreed.
