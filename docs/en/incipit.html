I will teach you Chi++; I will not show you the Way.
When someone shows you the Way, always remember that any way can be walked in two directions: one direction brings us closer to our destination; one direction takes us further away.
The direction matters, not the way.
If a man knows the direction, the way no longer matters.
C’hi++ is at once a programming language, a state of mind, and a way of life.
Whoever practises C’hi++ writes code as though living, and lives as though writing code.
Living is like paddling a canoe down a river.
Struggling to paddle upstream is useless and unproductive, because the river is stronger than us and never tires.
Letting yourself drift with the current is dangerous, because an obstacle might appear in front of you with no way to avoid it.
So one must paddle only as much as needed to move faster than the current and to dodge whatever obstacles appear along the way.
Likewise, writing good code means writing only the code necessary to reach the purpose you set out to achieve.
Adding a single extra comma is a mistake, because it makes debugging harder and the program slower.
Whoever practises C’hi++ applies the same principle to their life, performing only the necessary actions and ignoring everything superfluous.
Recognising which actions are necessary is simple, just as it’s simple to recognise the direction of the current when you’re navigating a river.
The hard part is realising you’re on a river.
Every cosmogony ever formulated throughout the centuries shares one trait: they all demand a certain amount of faith in something whose existence cannot be proven; one or more fictitious, supernatural elements introduced into the system to explain what cannot be practically demonstrated.
Believing in these entities requires accepting conditions more paradoxical than the ones they were originally meant to explain.
But, as Master Canaro always used to say: “the Truth is simple”, and any explanation requiring corrective parameters is, by definition, inexact.
What religions never say, however, is that no one goes to Paradise alone: either we all go, or none of us does.

C’hi, in Japanese, means “breath”.
The Universe is written in C’hi++.
The Universe breathes.
What we perceive as eternity is nothing but a single breath of the Universe, one of the countless eternities that have followed one another since the beginning of time.
Every breath of the Universe consists of a phase of expansion and a phase of contraction.
The endpoints of each phase entail the annihilation of every form of sentient life.
In the intervening periods, by contrast, countless worlds are born and die.
On each of these countless worlds, countless beings are born and die.
Each of these countless beings, over the course of its life, performs a series of actions that influence its own existence and that of its kind.
The possible permutations of these countless actions, for these countless beings, across these countless worlds, are almost unlimited.
Almost.
Time, on the other hand, is unlimited, so it can happen that a given sequence of events, having already occurred in a previous breath of the Universe, repeats itself, under similar conditions, in a subsequent breath.
Which means that, in all likelihood, you and I have already had this exact conversation in one, or in a thousand, of our previous existences.
