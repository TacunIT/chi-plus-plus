Numeral systems

There are 10 kinds of people: those who understand binary, and those who don't

To dissuade the few stubborn readers who have made it this far, I want to talk to you about numeral systems.

According to ancient legends, the human race is the result of a genetic engineering experiment carried out by six-fingered aliens who arrived on Earth three hundred thousand years before Christ.
Whether that’s true or not, imagine you have sitting at a table one of these aliens, one of your fellow brothers, and a man who has only one finger.
If you placed a dozen eggs on the table and asked each of them to count them, what would you get?
Your friend, who counts based on the ten fingers of his hands, would say the eggs equal all of his fingers once, plus two.
If we expressed this answer as powers of the number of fingers on your brother’s hands, we would get:

1x101 + 2x100 = 12

The alien, used to counting in base twelve, based on his twelve fingers, would say the eggs are:

1x121 + 0x120 = 10

The unfortunate one-fingered man, who can only consider the two possibilities finger/no finger, would be forced to do a longer calculation:

1x23 + 1x22 + 1x21 + 0x20 = 1100

These three results, however different, are not wrong, because each of the three counts the twelve eggs based on the number of their own fingers and answers accordingly. The eggs remain the same: only the numeral system changes.
It’s only natural that this should be so: numbers are just abstract concepts; symbols used to identify quantities.
You could replace the numbers from 0 to 9 with the first ten letters of the alphabet, and nothing would change:

0123456789
abcdefghil 
a + b = b     
b + c = d   
d * e = bc

The calculation method stays the same: when the quantity being evaluated is greater than the number of available digits, the difference is carried over to the left, into the higher-order column.
The value bc of the multiplication in fact means:

b*(number of digits)1 + 
c*(number of digits)0

that is, since the number of digits available to us is 10:

b*10 + c*1

If we substitute the letters with the numbers, we get:

1*10 + 2*1 = 3*4

Hexadecimal notation does exactly this: it uses the letters from A to F in addition to the ten values of Arabic numerals:

0123 4567 89AB CDEF

This turns out to be quite useful when you’re dealing with eight-digit bytes, as we’ll see later.

In positional numeral systems, the value of a digit depends on where it sits: digits on the left carry more weight than digits on the right, but not all numeral systems work this way.
If you asked an ancient Roman to work out the number of eggs, he would answer:

XII

This too would be a correct result, even though different from the previous ones.
The Roman numeral system is a bit more complicated than the base-10, base-12, and base-2 systems we’ve seen so far.
In Roman numerals, the value of a digit (I, V, X, L, C, D, M) doesn’t depend on its absolute position, but on its position relative to the other digits.
Master Canaro was born on February 29, 1964; in Roman numerals, this date is:

XXIX II MCMLXIV

that is:

10+10+(10-1)
 
1+1

1000+(1000-100)+50+10+(5-1)

Roman numerals may be fine for a people of conquerors: they’re easy to carve in marble, to commemorate a victorious battle, and they don’t account for zero, for nothingness — but for trade and calculations in general they’re rather inconvenient.
When the Arabs spread throughout the Mediterranean basin the numeral system they had in turn learned from the Indians, its spread was as rapid as it was inevitable.
Computers, however, are neither self-centered like a Roman emperor, nor cunning like a Phoenician merchant; on the contrary, they’re fast, but dim-witted: you have to explain things to them in a simple way, so they can carry them out.
Roman numerals aren’t for them, because they require too many symbols and too many rules to be put into practice.
Decimal notation is already a bit better, but it still requires the definition of ten different symbols, one for each digit from 0 to 9.
Paradoxically, the numeral system a computer can understand most easily is the one that’s most complicated for us: the binary system.
This system requires only the definition of two symbols: 1 and 0; two concepts that both a transistor and a punched card can easily grasp.

Someone might tell you there are better numeral systems than binary.
Someone, probably one of those unfortunate individuals who administer databases, might tell you that balanced ternary works much better and simplifies calculations.
This is true.
The reason Master Canaro did not approve of ternary systems was philosophical, not technical. The two values of the binary system can be identified with the logical values yes/no, true/false; that is, with the two conditions of existence and non-existence, for example, of a hole in a punched card or of a current in a circuit.
By contrast, systems with bases higher than two require the definition of a third state that can be neither yes nor no; neither true nor false; neither existent nor non-existent.
This third, intermediate state can only be defined by means of an evaluation that is not logical, but quantitative.
Suppose that on a punched card the value 0 is associated with the absence of holes and the value 1 is associated with the presence of a hole; a third value, different from 0 and from 1, could be identified either by a hole of a different size or by a dimple.
Whatever solution is chosen, we would be forced to define a value based on a physical quantity, that is, to base digital computation on an analog measurement.
Master Canaro thought this was impious.

An example of everything I’ve told you about today can be found in the RGB encoding of colors on Web pages.
In RGB encoding, each color is defined as a combination of red, green, and blue — the colors Red, Green, and Blue, whose initials make up the acronym of the system.
The amount of each color is determined by an 8-bit value and can therefore range from 0 to 255 (28).
