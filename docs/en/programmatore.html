The Good Programmer

It was an exhausting day: this morning I removed a comma from my code, and tonight I put it back

The main disciplines of computer science are: personnel management, project management, systems management, and programming.
A good master must be skilled in all disciplines, but can only excel in one of them, because reaching excellence requires devoting one’s entire life to that discipline, and no one has more than one life.
It is essential that the master know and practice all disciplines, because only by knowing the issues connected to each phase and each discipline can one achieve excellence in one of them.
The highest discipline is programming, because it has nothing to do with real entities, only with abstract concepts.
There is no difference between writing code and running a company: it is like observing the same city on maps of different scale.
If the scale ratio is low, you get a good overview but few details; if the scale ratio is high, you get many details but a limited overview.
The city, however, is always the same.
The Bhagavad-Gita says:

You have a right to action alone, never to its fruits: let not the fruit of action be your motive, nor let there be in you any attachment to inaction.1

This is how the good programmer operates: he writes good code, even though he will never benefit from its effects. He writes good code because he cannot help it.
Writing code is not a trade, it’s a mental deformity.
The good programmer is a misfit who has the good fortune of being paid to indulge the worst sides of his personality.
While writing code, the good programmer’s mind works on three levels: the first level decides the sequence of instructions needed to make the program work; the second level constantly checks its syntactic and semantic correctness; the third level checks whether there is a more efficient way to do the same thing.
Good programmers are paranoid, and rightly so.
The instructions of a program are executed millions of times over the course of their life, and even a minimal probability of error is unacceptable.
Remember: code works the way you want it to only if you leave it no other choice.
In times past there were those who held a bizarre theory according to which programs, to be truly effective, should have been fast, redundant, and imprecise.
The underlying idea was that, since it’s impossible, above a certain level of complexity, to produce error-free code, it would be better to produce many programs of average imprecision and evaluate the set of their results.
The only thing I can say in their defense is that in those days code production was thought of as an industrial activity, not as a mystical discipline.
The good systems administrator is paranoid too, but a good programmer will rarely be a good systems administrator.
The good systems administrator seeks redundancy, because redundancy increases the reliability of the system.
The good programmer shuns redundancy, because redundancy decreases the efficiency of the code.
The programmer is Mercury, the systems administrator is Vulcan.

A good programmer should know more than one programming language.
It is acceptable to know only one particular language in depth, but an overall view is always necessary in order to make choices.
Those who nowadays call themselves “programmers” are nothing more than convicts of a particular language.
Raised in a battery cage like chickens, they know only that one, consider it the best, and take an interest in other languages only in order to point out their flaws.
This is unproductive and dangerous, because as time passes these “programmers” will become analysts, and will manage the projects and the people entrusted to them with the same shortsightedness, producing systems that are unstable, expensive, and hard to maintain.
This is immoral.
At the opposite extreme are those who devote their time and energy to studying the languages and design methodologies of the past.
They can quote LISP, Ada, or Eiffel constructs with great precision, but have never written a single line of original code in their lives.
The most skilled among them manage to modify the sample programs they find in textbooks or manuals, swapping the algorithms for other pieces of classic code, but without any creative contribution.
Between these individuals and true programmers there is the same difference as between a sexologist and a porn star.

I once asked Master Canaro: “Master, what did paranoid people do before computer science was invented?” The Master thought about it for a while, then answered: “They moored boats.”
