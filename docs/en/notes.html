Notes

Notes are the most important part of a book. William Hazlitt, in the first chapter of On the Ignorance of the Learned, argues that those who read too many books have no ideas of their own, and are therefore forced to borrow other people’s wisdom; at the same time, with admirable consistency, in about eight pages of text, he makes a dozen citations: from the Gospel to Milton, from Shakespeare to Wordsworth. I would like you, then, to pause and reflect on why you’ve been moved to quote other people’s works here. If it’s a way of saying “See? He thought the same as me!”, that’s wrong; if anything, you should be saying “See? I think the same as him!” As I told you earlier, Truth is one; you can portray her in oils, in tempera, in charcoal, or in a single line, but the subject of your work will always be the same; if it isn’t, then it isn’t Her you’re talking about. René Guénon said:

It even seems that philosophers care far more about posing “problems” — however artificial and illusory they may be — than about solving them: this is one aspect of the disordered need for research for its own sake, that is, the vainest agitation, no less in the mental order than in the bodily one. These same philosophers are also concerned with attaching their name to a “system” — that is, to a strictly delimited set of theories that is their own and signifies nothing but their own work. Hence the desire to be original at any cost, even if truth must be sacrificed to such originality. For a philosopher’s reputation, inventing a new error is worth far more than repeating a truth already expressed by others. This form of individualism, to which so many mutually contradictory (if not self-contradictory) “systems” are owed, is nonetheless found in equal measure among modern scientists and artists. But it is perhaps among philosophers that the intellectual anarchy which is its inevitable consequence stands out most clearly.1

This need to stand out, this restlessness, are clear symptoms of servitude to Entropy — permissible for a kshatriya, but unacceptable for a brâhmana. Just as Faulkner’s woods belonged neither to Major De Spain, nor to Thomas Sutpen, from whom he’d bought them, nor to Chickasaw chief Ikkemotubbe2, who had sold them to him, Linux belongs neither to its developers, nor to Linus Torvalds3, nor even to Andrew Tanenbaum4. In the same way, you can’t say C’hi++ belongs to you, because you described it in your book, nor to me, because I taught it to you, nor to Master Canaro, who in turn taught it to me. As happens with software development, each of us has, in his own way, described the same model, adding something, correcting something, and, inevitably, introducing new mistakes, which will have to be fixed by whoever comes after us. There’s nothing wrong with any of this. It isn’t mistakes you should be afraid of, but stagnation.
