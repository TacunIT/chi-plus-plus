C’hi++ is an excellent programming manual, a clumsy philosophy book, and a disgraceful physics book.

Besides being a programming manual, C’hi++ is also a book that tries to explain the meaning of Existence. The pairing isn’t as far-fetched as it seems: just as religions define the relationship between man and the Divine, programming languages define how programs relate to the operating system, and quite often the principles that hold in one domain can also be used in the other. Loading data and programs into a computer’s RAM, for example, is the computational transposition of the Cycle of Rebirth found in Eastern religions, while C++ classes mirror the Aristotelian concepts of universal and substance.

C’hi++ does not present itself as an absolute and definitive explanation of the Meaning of Existence, but as an incomplete and perfectible model. This is precisely why it has a version number, like software: so that it can evolve over time, the same way software does. Just as the mathematical models used for meteorology don’t capture the full complexity of the atmosphere’s air-mass movements, but still provide reliable forecasts, so C’hi++ does not offer a complete and accurate description of what is, was, and will be, but it can offer a non-dogmatic answer to some questions about our life.

C’hi++ is neither science nor religion, even though it sometimes pretends to be one or the other.

C’hi++ is myth, and mythology has often blended the supernatural with the highest level of scientific or technological development of its time. In antiquity, we attributed a divine counterpart to the lightning that generated fire, to the motion of the stars, to the weather. More recently, Poe, in Eureka, hypothesized that the Universe is the result of the opposing action of Gravity and Electricity.

At the start of the last century, finally, the Futurists mythologized the symbols of technological progress — speed, propellers, airplanes, automobiles —, making them the subject of their art. So it’s nothing strange that at the start of this millennium someone would attempt a comparison between the known rules of software and the unknown rules of the Universe we live in.

The text is inspired by The Book of Five Rings, by Miyamoto Musashi (1645), and is imagined as having been written by a young disciple transcribing the teachings of his Master on the Art of programming.

The two live in a monastery and belong to a religious/computing Order founded by the mythical Master Canaro who, according to legend, had been tasked by God with rewriting in C++ the application that runs the Universe, and who, following that experience, defined a doctrine called C’hi++, capable of explaining the meaning of Existence without any metaphysical element — or almost.

The first three chapters of the book introduce the characters and the setting in which the story takes place; the chapters from the fourth to the twenty-first each illustrate an aspect of C++ programming; the final chapter tells of the Master’s death and his last teaching to his disciple.

Each technical chapter of the book is divided into two parts: the first explains an aspect of the language or of programming, the second illustrates one of the aspects of C’hi++, usually related to the chapter’s own technical topic.

Beyond its novelistic and not always serious presentation, C’hi++ is a model that does a fairly good job of the task it was conceived for, namely: providing an explanation of Existence without resorting to metaphysical elements, but relying only on what we have direct experience of.

Its principles rest on a cosmogony, called Spazionism, which draws on some ideas from Edgar Allan Poe’s essay Eureka, in particular the contrast between contraction and expansion, adding a greater level of detail by defining the hypothetical elements that make up the Universe, the so-called spazioni: infinitesimal elements endowed with “potential existence,” of which every element of the Universe is made.

For C’hi++, just as a computer screen is made up of a two-dimensional matrix of pixels, generally off, but able to light up when struck by a beam of light, the Universe is made up of a three-dimensional matrix of spazioni, able to acquire mass and therefore exist if they receive energy.

The life of the Universe is seen by C’hi++ as a continuous cycle, in which two universal Forces are opposed to one another: Gravity, that is, the tendency of everything that exists to reunite in the primordial One, and Entropy, understood here as a Force that tends to separate everything that exists. Because of this, every cycle of the Universe’s existence is made up of three phases:

Phase 1. The universe is empty, all energy is concentrated in a single point, held together by Gravity.

Phase 2. Entropy overwhelms Gravity and causes the One to explode. Energy begins to expand into the Universe, irradiating the spazioni, which thus acquire mass and existence, generating the stars and planets as we know them.

Phase 3. When the push given by Entropy decreases, Gravity reverses the motion of energy/matter; the Universe contracts, returning to concentrate itself in the One. When all the Universe’s energy is once again concentrated in the One, the cycle begins again.

Spazioni aside, the only act of faith that C’hi++ asks of its followers concerns the fact that the Universe, after a cycle of expansion, will surely go back to collapsing in on itself, only to explode again. When that happens, there are two possible cases:

Case 1. It could be that a Big Bang only happens under certain conditions, and that those conditions necessarily lead to a Universe identical to the one we know now; so, if the One explodes again, everything will start over from the beginning.

Case 2. If every Big Bang happens under specific circumstances and in specific ways, when the One explodes again, a new Universe will be born, one that may have few or no points of contact with the current one.

The first hypothesis is possible, but unlikely, so C’hi++ takes for granted that the correct one is the second. On the other hand, however low the probability may be that two identical explosions occur, over an infinite span of time we cannot rule out that this Universe will manifest itself again, and that what exists within it, ourselves included, may also come to exist again. Since our conscious self can manifest now here, now there, depending on the case, if in this cycle of the Universe I harm someone, in another cycle of this particular kind of Universe I might find myself as one of my own victims, suffering the consequences of my own actions. It is therefore preferable that I behave well, and that I try to convince others to do the same. The problem, as always, is understanding what “good” really means. To do that, we need to introduce the only truly metaphysical element of C’hi++: a kind of persistent memory of the Universe — similar to a computer’s hard drive, or to the Vāsanā of Yoga and Buddhism — in which a trace remains of the choices we’ve made over the course of our existences, and of the consequences they’ve produced. If we understand that one of our actions had negative consequences, that action will be “stored” among our mistakes, in the hope that it won’t be repeated on other occasions. Conversely, actions that had positive outcomes will be “remembered” as good, so that they may be repeated in other cycles of the Universe’s life.

In summary, for C’hi++, every sentient being is one of the neurons of a brain (the Universe) which it helps shape through what it learns over the course of its existences. If we recognize our mistakes as such, it’s possible we won’t repeat them in the future; otherwise we’ll keep making them, generating “painful” forms of the Universe. For this reason, according to C’hi++, “the meaning of Life is debugging,” because only by analyzing and correcting our errors, the same way it’s done with software, can we improve the “shape” of the Universe and, as a consequence, our existences. In other words, Heaven and Hell are not elsewhere: they are just two possible states of the Universe, and it is we, through our actions and our intentions, who decide which of the two we will live in.
