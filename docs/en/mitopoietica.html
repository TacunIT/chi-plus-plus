Mythopoetics of C'hi++

On the seventh day, God made a backup

There are two theories about me: some think I went mad trying to debug the Universe, others think I decided to debug the Universe because I’m mad.

Master Canaro too was accused of being mad when he said God had tasked him with porting the Universe from COBOL to C++, but he wasn’t mad.
I’ve kept these documents: they are the last page of his diary and the reports that were sent to the company he worked for while he was on assignment to the “Client”.
Put them at the beginning of your book, so that everything we say afterward will be nothing but a repetition.

Last page of Master Canaro's diary (April 8)

I'm at the office, working, when the phone rings. The voice that answers is rather deep and has a curious echo. It says:
- Canaro, it's God, I need you.
- Marco, I'm busy, talk later.
I hang up. A moment later the phone rings again. This time I pay attention to the type of ring and deduce it's an outside call.
- Canaro, I'm not your colleague, I really am God. I need you to help me port the universe to C++.
I can't help but smile.
- Cute. And the voice synthesizer seems to work pretty well too. Look, let me finish this report and I'll buy you a coffee, but let me work for now.
I hang up again, and again the wretched thing makes its voice heard. It's an outside line, I can't risk it: it could be a client, but I already know that..
- Canaro, listen to me once and for all: I really am God and I need you to solve a problem for me.
I'm about to say something I'll regret, but the voice goes on:
- If you don't believe me, unplug the phone.
- Oh, come on..
- Unplug it!
More out of curiosity than anything else, I decide to call the bluff. I unplug it.
- Do you believe me now?
I have to admit that for a moment my skepticism wavers. I look for a possible explanation.
- You put a receiver in the handset.
- Stubborn, eh? All right: pick a random object.
- To do what?
- To use as a handset, one your colleague couldn't have tampered with.
- Marco, please..
- Come on, what does it cost you? Since you're so sure..
- Is my pipe okay?
- Whatever you like: hold it up to your ear.
Let's see where he's going with this: I take my pipe out of its leather case and hold the bowl up to my ear.
- Done, - I say, and meanwhile I look around, waiting for the prank.
- I know, - the voice is in the briar bowl. - Now, if we could please get back to our phone conversation.. I wouldn't want someone to walk in and see you talking to a pipe.

- Let me start by saying I don't understand anything about these computer things, -  he tells me as soon as I've plugged the phone back in.
- My son, on the other hand, knows how to use computers, and he told me it would be better to port the universe to an object-oriented language, suggesting this C++. Now, you see, I'm strong in the natural sciences, not in computer science, and all these technical terms are a mystery to me: what does it mean to port something? And what are object-oriented languages?
“Good question,” I thought. Years earlier I had written a C++ manual, and the object-oriented paradigm was the subject of the first chapter. If only he had read it..
- I also skimmed through your book, but I didn't understand a thing: you write terribly, let me tell you. And then, I thought, who knows how many things have changed in the meantime, so I decided to call you.
- First of all, thank you for the compliments, and then, to answer your question, porting something means rewriting an application in a programming language different from the one it was originally written in.
- And what's the purpose of that?
- Usually to take advantage of the new language's capabilities, or to be able to use the application on a system where the old language isn't supported.
- Like translating the ten commandments from Hebrew into Latin?
- Something like that.
- And this “C++”? What does “object-oriented language” mean?
- That instead of abstract data types like numbers and characters, it lets you use data types more similar to real things.
- I don't understand.
- Old programming languages forced the programmer to use only predefined data types: numbers, letters, dates. That's fine for a certain kind of application, but it makes it hard to write more complex applications where it can help to have a language capable of handling new, user-defined data types as well. In C++, the language your son recommended to you, this is achieved by means of classes.
- Like in zoology?
- Sort of. By class we mean a non-standard data type, for which you can define the behavior.
- Give me an example, I don't get it. And also, enough with this formal “you”, let's switch to the informal one, it's simpler.
- Let's say you have two strings..
- I don't wear shoes with strings, I wear Birkenstock sandals.
- I meant sequences of characters. Now, in the way a computer normally handles data, it makes no sense to add two strings together, since it's not a numeric data type, but you can define a “String” class that, if subjected to addition, does something sensible.
- Like what?
- Suppose you have any string A.
- Is “Foo” okay?
- Original. And suppose you want to join it to another string B.
- “Bar”.
- Also quite original. Anyway, you would write the instruction: C = A + B, and string C would contain: “FooBar”.
- I'll keep that in mind the next time I need to write “FooBar”.
- It was an example.
- I still don't get why they're called “object-oriented languages”.
- Because they don't handle raw data, but objects with a well-defined behavior of their own.
- I need to see it in practice. Anyway, what would be the advantages of this translation?
- In the case of C++, easier analysis.
- But the analysis of the application already exists.
- And easier debugging.
- Sorry?
- Fixing errors.
- The current application has been running forever and has never given any problems.
- It also increases the chances of reusing the code in other applications.
- That sounds about as useful as the string “FooBar”.
- Does the production process have a quality certification?
- I saw that it was good: I'd say that's enough.
- Well, then I don't see any need to rewrite it.
- That's exactly what I told my son, but he started talking about this C++ and that other thing, the one named after an island, what's it called..
- Java? It's not named after an island, but after a type of coffee blend.
- Whatever it is. The point is: what's the need to rewrite everything?
- As far as I can tell, I'd say none..
- Good, thank you.
He hangs up (or whatever it is), but after a while he calls back.
- Canaro, I talked to my son and he says I'm old-fashioned, that the current application is obsolete, that users are complaining, and that this port is necessary. I don't understand anything anymore, what should I do?
I'm about to answer “God only knows”, but fortunately I hold back and resort to the standard answer for these cases:
- We could do a feasibility study and then decide..
- Sounds like a good idea. When can you start?
- I don't know, I have to ask my manager.
- All right: ask him and let me know.
He hangs up without telling me how I'm supposed to “let him know”, but that's the least of my problems. The first one, right now, is how to tell my boss about this without being taken for a madman.

- If you're hoping to get some sick leave for a nervous breakdown, forget it. You've said (and above all done) far stranger things.
- I know it's crazy, but it's true: God called me and asked me to rewrite the Universe in C++.
- Canaro, cut it out and go finish the analysis, we have to bring it to the client this afternoon.
- And if he calls me again, what should I tell him?
- Tell him how badly you got the estimates wrong on the last project, so he'll go to someone else.
Defeated, I go back to my cubbyhole, where I get my second pipe of the day ready ahead of time. As I'm fiddling with the tobacco, the phone rings for the umpteenth time, but it's not who I expect, it's my boss calling me into his office. “A summons from the king rarely bodes well..”, I think, but when I get to the well-known room with its unmistakable smell of Tuscan cigar, I find myself facing a man in a very different mood from the one who dismissed me a little while ago. His voice is calm, his manner kind, and he shows the utmost understanding and willingness to help me.
- Hand over what you're working on to Igino and Paolo, and devote yourself entirely to the feasibility study for the porting of the Universe. Astonished by the sudden about-face, I ask the first thing that comes to mind.
- Which job number do I charge my hours to?
- I don't know yet, but don't worry about it. Oh, if you need a laptop you can take mine.
- All right, thanks. Anything else?
- Here, - he hands me a slip of paper with something written on it. - he said you can call him back at this number.
I go back to my office, close the door, and dial the number: a recorded voice tells me the user is currently busy, but that a call-waiting notice has been sent. In no time at all I hear His voice say:
- Yes, hello?
- Sorry, I called you right back, but if you're on the phone..
- Don't worry, we were wrapping up. So?
- I'm at your disposal, but before we start there's something I need to tell you.
- You want to know how I convinced your boss? It was easy, I told him that taking things too seriously and getting angry shortens your life.
- I think he already knew that..
- Probably, but I told him by how much.
- You know when he'll die?
- Theoretically yes, but who keeps track.. I fired off a random date before the end of the year. I also told him this was his last chance to make sure that little twinge he felt in his chest this morning when he woke up was just a little twinge. That was enough.
- At least it seems to have worked. Anyway, that's not what I wanted to ask you.
- Oh! You probably want to know why I chose you of all people, when I had the best available.
- I would have said “among the best”, but anyway..
- I'll tell you if you do a good job. We'll meet tomorrow morning at the address your boss gave you. Try to be on time, and don't go through downtown, there'll be a line because of a fender-bender.
- I'll remember that.

First progress report on the “U++” project (April 19)

Things are going better than I'd hoped: despite his commitments, the Client always finds time for me and my work is proceeding briskly. Moreover, despite a poorly hidden initial reluctance, I believe he's finally become convinced of the usefulness of a code review, and now it's He himself, and no longer the Son, who's pushing for a swift conclusion. So far there have been two discoveries of major importance: the first is that the current application is written in COBOL (the Client explained to me that the initial idea of using FORTRAN had been abandoned after a first attempt, and that only the engineers' brains were written in that language); the second is that Mandelbrot was right: the Universe has a fractal structure (a choice which greatly simplifies the handling of complex situations such as the interaction between weather agents, the noise of pedal boats, and chance encounters with ex-lovers at the department store). A proposal of mine that met with the Client's approval is to proceed in stages, drafting a complete general analysis but rewriting the application little by little, so as to cause the least possible disruption to users and to simplify the work of fine-tuning the procedures. I wrote a preliminary report (attached) and handed it to the Client: tomorrow he'll give me his opinion on it.

Second report on the “U++” project (April 20)

This morning I discussed my preliminary report with the Client, who on the whole seemed satisfied with the approach chosen. After some hesitation, he also became convinced that it's not possible to compare the development times of the previous application and the new one, both because structured analysis and object-oriented analysis can't be compared, and because of the difficulty of comparing the current measurement, in man-days, with the previous one, in God-days. At the moment I have only one concern, namely that someone, behind the scenes, is steering the Client's moods for unclear purposes. I say this because all the doubts and misgivings he has expressed so far are far beyond his technical knowledge. If at first I thought that behind all this was that Son I already mentioned in the memo I sent you before my departure (which would put me in an extremely delicate position, since, as we all well know, my very presence here is due precisely to his suggestion, and calling his competence into question would be counterproductive), I am now reasonably certain that it's someone else, although I have no idea either who it might be or what his purposes might be.

Third report on the “U++” project (April 28)

I apologize for the prolonged silence, but the meetings of the last few days have been absolutely grueling, and I haven't had time to keep you updated on the most recent developments. On the other hand, I'm finally starting to get a somewhat clearer picture of the various functional requirements and the roles involved in the project, which is certainly a good thing, especially since the ground here is starting to burn under my feet. And I don't say that lightly. As you surely remember, in my previous communication I had raised the suspicion of hidden interference, and in any case a doubt I'd had from the start was: given that the Client has repeatedly stated he has no great technical knowledge of computer science, who originally wrote the first application? Certainly not the Son (who is nothing more than a dabbler with a smattering of secondhand knowledge picked up from trade magazines), nor the third member of the board of directors, who is almost always traveling. So, who? Well: now I have an answer to both questions, and good reason to suspect that the two figures (the hidden operator and the author of the code) are one and the same, but I don't know whether to be pleased or frightened about it. In any case, it's better to tell you everything from the beginning. The Client decides to build an application to manage the Universe (which is the one currently in production), so he sits down at his desk, drafts the functional analysis, adds a few business rules (such as: water always flows downward, everyone has only one mother, and so on), then moves on to implementation. He starts with something small and easy, but the result he gets doesn't satisfy him; moreover, like many creative types, he's not particularly drawn to the practical realization of what he's worked out conceptually, so he decides to subcontract the work to third parties, creating them on the spot. The newcomers study the analysis, define the database design, establish some implementation criteria (they switch from FORTRAN to COBOL), and finally start writing the code. The analysis, needless to say, is extremely precise, and everything would go perfectly if, at a certain point, one of the programmers didn't start inserting backdoors into the program. It starts almost as a joke (he modifies the code to guarantee himself invincibility at cards and ownership of Boardwalk and Park Place in Monopoly), but then he gets a taste for it and starts adding increasingly substantial changes, drawing some of his colleagues into the corruption as well. In the end, his privileges are only slightly lower than those of the System Administrator. Emboldened by the impunity he's enjoyed until then, he takes charge of his band of scoundrels and attempts to overthrow the Client in a coup, but without success: his plan is discovered immediately, and both he and his followers are transferred, as punishment, to a secondary site, an open-plan office with no air conditioning, where it gets absurdly hot in summer. They're saved from a worse fate (the presence, in the office, of female colleagues) by a curious circumstance: they're needed for the maintenance of the program. Indeed, even though the conspiracy is foiled, the damage to the code remains, and there's neither time to recheck everything nor anyone else who could do it. So it's decided to put the application into production as it is, and to remove the anomalies as they come to light: a choice as risky as it is necessary, one that soon leads to a paradoxical situation in which the conspirators become the Client's go-to people for fixing the errors that they themselves generated and continue to generate (partly out of revenge, partly to increase their own bargaining power), exploiting the illegal privileges they've secured for themselves. It's easy to understand how little joy these characters felt at my arrival: although apparently masochistic, the Client's choice to rewrite the entire application now turns out to be the only remaining chance to eliminate Evil from Creation. So much for the external interference, so much for the reason behind it. Wish me luck.

Fourth report on the “U++” project (May 1)

Believe me: good clients only exist for salespeople; for technicians, there are only clients who haven't (yet) caused problems. I was so thrilled at the idea of being the Divine Instrument that would eliminate Evil from the universe, and instead here I am, sunk in the darkest depression because of the Client and his absurd fixations. I thought, in fact I was certain, that in the new version of the application the watchword would be “Good”: imagine my surprise when I discovered that, even in this version, existence will be a seesaw between the hills of moderate contentment and the oceanic depths of melancholy. This morning I talked to the Client about it, tried to reason with him. I even got Schopenhauer and Bergson to lend a hand, since they were here anyway and had nothing else to do, but it was no use. He answered me, annoyed, to do my job and not meddle in matters beyond my understanding (only to then coax me into helping him with backups after he'd accidentally deleted two galaxies). In the afternoon I went back on the attack, convinced as I was that the considerable quantities of vin santo he'd downed after lunch would have made him more receptive, but I was wrong: he repeated what he'd already said in the morning and, on top of that, gave me a vaguely Taoist-flavored lecture, whose declared purpose was to enlighten me on the subjectivity of the concepts of good and evil. In one last, desperate attempt, I pointed out that the presence of Evil in Creation is one of the favorite arguments of those who try to prove that God doesn't exist, but it backfired: he confessed to me that it was precisely he who had planted such thoughts in the minds of his detractors, in the frustrated hope that people would find someone else to curse instead. Once again, I find myself facing the age-old dichotomy between what the client wants and what he actually needs, and once again it will be the thankless task of the computer scientist to appear to fall short of his own duties in order to produce something suited to the client's real needs.

Client's fax (May 19)

This is to inform you of our decision to discontinue the porting of our management system to the C++ language; a painful but irrevocable decision, which we have reached having found that the main flaws of the previous application had not been remedied in this new version. Given this, we have decided to focus our efforts, rather than on a risky revision project, on an organic user-training program, in the hope that a greater awareness of the underlying issues of the context of use may correct what, at this point, we consider to be congenital idiosyncrasies of the system. As a reward for the effort you have put into this project, we thought of sparing the building housing your company from the earthquake of July 24th, but we are open to any solution of your liking, provided it does not run counter to our corporate policies. Unfortunately, your staff seconded to us fell victim to the age-old dichotomy between what the users of a system want and what they actually need, and we trust you will have no objection if we use him as a guinea pig for our re-education program. At present, however, he is in excellent health and seems to have no difficulty integrating with the new team he has been assigned to, at our secondary data-center site.

Once the retraining course was over, Master Canaro was never the same again.
He went back to his company, but stayed only long enough to hand in his resignation.
He founded our Order and began preaching the mysticism of programming, immediately gaining a large following.
The time was ripe for change: there had been the prodigious growth of the end of the millennium, and there had been the ruinous crash of March 2000. The old guard was tired of depending on the whims of the stock market and of marketing; the young wanted the Promised Land for which they had abandoned classical studies; users just wanted their email to work.
Master Canaro’s doctrine was simple: bring computing back into an ivory tower, accept its imponderable component, and raise it from a productive activity to a mystical one.
Enough with improvisation, enough with time to market, enough with unreliable products: whoever wanted to write code had to enter a monastic Order and follow a long path of initiation before they could begin working.
Many saw in Master Canaro “the wind that would sweep away the mud”: the old guard was delighted to see its status as a chosen caste recognized once again; the young, who were looking for shelter, found it within the monasteries; users learned that their email would work, and were happy.
There was also a political component that favored the success of the doctrine preached by Master Canaro. Society had by then become entirely dependent on computers, and computers depended on computer scientists.
If they had ever decided to band together and go on strike, they could have brought the country, if not the whole world, to its knees.
The psychopathologies typical of computer scientists, their inability to manage — or even to conceive of — a social life, made this hypothesis quite unlikely, but it was still too great a risk to ignore.
On the contrary, the indoctrination of computer scientists, their segregation into a caste with strong mystical-religious overtones, would keep them away from the temptations of trade unions and would make the prospect of a strike as improbable as a strike of priests.

Master Canaro’s teachings never strayed beyond the realm of software.
He never once mentioned hardware in any of his writings; he used to say that if a man configures a firewall he is a computer scientist, if he connects two cables he is an electrician.
Once I asked Master Canaro why he had never given any guidance on the physical reliability of servers.
“If it comes to that,” he answered, “I never produced any tables for calculating reinforced concrete either.” Only years later did I understand what he meant.
Despite his lack of interest in what he called “real things”, the cultural revolution brought about by Master Canaro had consequences for computer manufacturers too.
Most of them were slow to perceive the changes underway and paid dearly for their inattention; the others thrived.
Even though Master Canaro never took a definite side in the dispute that pitted supporters of the doctrine of the Great Vehicle (the mainframes) against those who instead favored the Small Vehicle (home PCs), some high prelates declared themselves in favor of mainframe monotheism, and in the end the supporters of polytheism were defeated.
In offices and homes, PCs were replaced by terminals, and every building, besides the garage and the boiler room, now had a server room.
The territory was divided into dioceses, and each diocese was entrusted to a deacon whose task was to oversee the correct functioning of the various systems.
Although those of us who had been close to him since the beginning of his preaching knew that C++ was his favorite programming language, Master Canaro let Java become the community’s official language, quietly enduring the attacks of those who accused him of having sold out to Sun.
The existing procedures were all rewritten; the monastic orders that taught C++ became fewer and fewer, until eventually there were none left.
It was only then that Master Canaro spoke to me for the first time about C’hi++.

The clouds that darkened the sky this morning have vanished, the night sky overflows with stars.
These are things one notices, as one grows older.
I have noticed them since I was a child.

What I will teach you is not illegal, but it is as if it were.
